Prime Numbers - Gauss Staircase

The Prime Number Theorem describes the asymptotic distribution of prime numbers. The prime-counting function, denoted π(x), gives the number of primes less than or equal to x.

This visualization compares the actual step function π(x) (in blue) with the logarithmic integral approximation Li(x) (in red), illustrating the famous Riemann Hypothesis regarding the distribution of zeros of the zeta function.

Fibonacci Sequence - Visualization

The Fibonacci sequence is defined by the recurrence: F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2), with F(0)=0 and F(1)=1.

This sequence is intimately linked to the Golden Ratio (φ ≈ 1.618). The visualization shows the logarithmic spiral (golden spiral) built from squares whose sides follow the Fibonacci sequence, a pattern often observed in nature (shells, sunflowers).

Pascal's Triangle

Pascal's Triangle is a geometric presentation of binomial coefficients. Each number is the sum of the two numbers directly above it.

By coloring odd numbers and leaving even ones white, a fractal structure known as Sierpinski's Triangle appears. This property links combinatorial arithmetic to fractal geometry.

Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot Set is the set of points c in the complex plane for which the sequence defined by z₀=0 and z(n+1) = z(n)² + c remains bounded.

It is the archetype of fractals: an infinitely complex structure exhibiting self-similarity at different scales. The boundary of the set is infinitely complex, revealing 'seahorse' patterns and miniature copies of the main set.

Ulam Spiral

The Ulam Spiral (or prime spiral) is a simple method of representing prime numbers that reveals unexpected patterns.

By writing positive integers in a square spiral and marking prime numbers, one observes that they tend to align on diagonals. This phenomenon, discovered by Stanislaw Ulam in 1963, suggests non-random properties in the distribution of prime numbers.

Weierstrass Function

The Weierstrass Function is a famous counter-example in mathematical analysis: it is a function that is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere.

Defined as an infinite trigonometric series, it presents a fractal structure: no matter the zoom level, the curve remains 'rough' and never smooths out into a straight line, defying classical intuition of differential calculus.

Parametric 3D Surface

This visualization represents a parametric 3D surface defined by complex mathematical equations (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v)).

The interactive graph allows exploring the topology of the surface, showing how simple trigonometric functions can combine to create organic shapes and complex undulations in three-dimensional space.
